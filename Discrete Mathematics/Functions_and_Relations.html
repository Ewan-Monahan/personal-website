Functions and Relations


Before we learn about relations and functions, we must realised that items or 'elements' can be grouped in ordered or unordered fashion.

Unordered pairs are denoted by square brackets, $[a,b]$.
Ordered pairs, however, are denoted inside round brackets $(a,b)$.
In the case of unordered pairs, $[a,b] = [b,a], [j,z] = [z,j], [2,5] = [5,2]$ and so forth.
When it comes to ordered pairs, order matters! $(a,b) \neq (b,a)$
An example of ordered pairs are coordinates on an x,y plane. We know that the coordinates (3,5) are very different to (5,3).

A relation $R$ between sets $A$ and $B$ is a set of ordered pairs $(a,b)$ where $a \in A$, $b \in B$. It is a subset of $A \times B$
A function is a relation in which no element of the domain appears more than once as a first ordinate.

Or more formally,
A relation is a function $\iff (x,y) \in R$ and $(x,z) \in R \implies y=z$
